DSC 106 — Data Visualization Ethics
Proposition: "Limited access to abortion providers leads to lower abortion rates."
================================================================================


================================================================================
VISUALIZATION 1: SUPPORTS THE PROPOSITION
Title: "States With Fewer Providers Show Lower Abortion Rates"
================================================================================

This is a scatterplot where X = percent of counties without an abortion clinic
(2020), Y = abortions per 1,000 women aged 15–44 (2020), and a red OLS
regression line ties the relationship together visually.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 1 — Rate Measured "By State of Occurrence," Not "By Residence"
Score: -2.0  (fully deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The abortion rate plotted on the Y-axis counts abortions that physically
occurred inside each state — not abortions obtained by that state's residents.
That is a huge distinction. In states with very few clinics, many residents
drive or fly to a neighboring state for care. Their abortions then get counted
in the neighboring state's occurrence total, not their home state's. So a state
like Mississippi looks like it has a very low abortion rate on this chart — and
it does, by occurrence — but a meaningful fraction of that low number reflects
women who simply had to travel, not women who couldn't or didn't get abortions.
The "by residence" rate (which follows the woman, not the clinic) would be the
more honest measure of actual abortion incidence. I deliberately chose
occurrence because it makes the correlation appear cleaner and more dramatic.
The alternative — plotting by-residence rate — was tried and produced a similar
negative correlation (r ≈ -0.58 vs. r ≈ -0.52 for occurrence), but the
by-occurrence version produces more extreme low-end values for restricted states,
which makes the scatterplot look more convincing.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 2 — Truncated Y-Axis (starts at 2, not 0)
Score: -1.5  (mostly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The Y-axis floor is set to 2 rather than 0. This exaggerates the
apparent steepness of the regression line without altering a single data point.
Visually, the distance between the highest-rate states (~29) and lowest-rate
states (~3) looks much larger proportionally because the bottom of the axis
cuts away empty space — but it also cuts away the visual anchor at zero that
would remind the viewer how moderate these differences actually are. A Y-axis
starting at 0 would make the regression line look noticeably flatter and the
differences between states less dramatic. The APA and most data journalism
style guides recommend starting quantitative axes at zero; departing from that
default here is a deliberate choice to amplify the apparent effect. The one
fig-leaf justification is that no state has a rate below 3, so "the axis starts
near the data," but that framing is exactly the kind of rationalization that
makes this technique easy to deploy without feeling dishonest.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 3 — Red Color Gradient Encoding Restriction Level
Score: -1.0  (moderately deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: Each dot is colored on a white-to-dark-red gradient, where darker red
means a higher percentage of counties lack a clinic. This double-encodes the
X-axis information (you can read restriction level twice: once from position,
once from color). More importantly, it does so using red — a color with strong
negative cultural connotations. Darker red dots cluster in the lower-right
quadrant (high restriction, low abortion rate), which trains the eye to
associate that corner of the chart with alarm. An honest alternative would have
been a neutral gradient (gray to blue, or a colorblind-safe sequential palette
with no emotional charge). I chose red specifically because it makes the
restricted-access states feel more salient and threatening, which nudges viewers
toward a "something is wrong here" interpretation without requiring any explicit
claim to that effect.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 4 — Selective State Annotations
Score: -1.0  (moderately deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: Six states are labeled by name: Mississippi, Arkansas, and Wyoming (very
high restriction, low rate) and New York, New Jersey, and California (low
restriction, high rate). These are the states that sit closest to the extreme
corners of the trend, making the regression line look nearly inevitable. States
that don't follow the pattern — Nevada and Florida, for example, have fairly
high restriction but moderate-to-high rates — are left as anonymous dots in the
cluster. Selective annotation is one of the quietest forms of cherry-picking in
visualization: you're technically showing all the data, but you're directing the
viewer's attention only to the examples that confirm your argument. An honest
alternative would have been to label states randomly, label all 50, or label
specifically the outliers that challenge the trend. I did the opposite.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 5 — Removing Washington D.C. as an "Outlier"
Score: -0.5  (mildly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: DC was dropped from Visualization 1 with the stated reason that it's
a unique urban environment (no rural counties, no state legislature, no
comparable geography). That reasoning isn't totally wrong — DC is genuinely
anomalous in many ways. But the actual effect of removing it is that a data
point where access is at its maximum (0% of counties without a clinic) and the
rate is very high (~26 abortions per 1,000 women) gets silently excluded. If DC
were included, it would sit in the upper-left corner of the scatterplot and
noticeably reduce the slope of the regression line. Keeping DC would have made
the chart less visually convincing for the proposition. The "methodological"
justification was chosen after noticing its visual effect — not before — which
is what makes this mildly deceptive rather than merely careful.


================================================================================
VISUALIZATION 2: REFUTES THE PROPOSITION
Title: "Differences in Abortion Rates Across Access Levels Are Nearly Negligible"
================================================================================

This is a horizontal bar chart where states are collapsed into two coarse access
categories — High Access (0–49% of counties without a clinic) and Low Access
(50–100%) — and each bar shows the filtered group mean after selective outlier
removal. Individual state data points are not shown. ±1 standard-deviation error
bars extend from each bar end, and numeric mean labels appear in small gray text
offset past the error bar caps. The x-axis runs from 0 to 100. A bold title and
muted italic subtitle frame the chart as confirming the "nearly negligible"
conclusion before the viewer reads any data.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 1 — Coarse, Wide Binning (2 Groups Instead of Continuous Scale)
Score: -1.5  (mostly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The continuous variable "% of counties without a clinic" (which ranges
from 0 to 99 and has a clear negative Pearson correlation of r ≈ -0.58 with
abortion rate) is collapsed into just two buckets: 0–49% (High Access) and
50–100% (Low Access). This is the central structural deception of Visualization
2. On a scatterplot the negative trend is visually unmistakable. Folded into two
blunt bins, the continuous relationship disappears inside the groups. The Low
Access bucket alone contains roughly 36 states spanning an abortion-rate range of
approximately 4 to 19 per 1,000 women — enormous within-group variance that makes
access level appear to predict almost nothing. Using two groups rather than three
is more aggressive than a three-bin version: the single threshold at 50% throws
the widest possible net around the high-access states while maximizing heterogen-
eity in the low-access bucket. An honest alternative would have shown a scatter-
plot, or at minimum quartile bins that preserve the distribution's structure.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 2 — Extended X-Axis (0 to 100, Data Maximum Is ~29)
Score: -1.5  (mostly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The X-axis runs from 0 to 100, even though the highest state abortion rate
in the dataset is about 29. That pushes roughly 70% of the axis into empty white
space, compressing every visible difference into a narrow strip on the far left.
After the selective outlier removal in Decisions 6 and 8, the two group means
land at 14.8 (High Access) and 11.0 (Low Access) — a gap of 3.8 units. On a
0–30 scale that gap would occupy about 13% of the axis width and look clearly
meaningful. On a 0–100 scale it occupies less than 4%, and the two bars appear
almost the same length at a glance. A viewer who wants to compare the groups
precisely must locate the small gray mean labels, read off both values, and
subtract — exactly the kind of active effort most readers will not bother with.
An honest alternative would set the axis limit at or just above the data maximum
(~30), which is standard practice for bar charts of rates.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 3 — Error Bars Framed as ±1 SD to Suggest Group Overlap
Score: -1.5  (mostly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: Each bar carries error whiskers labeled as ±1 standard deviation. Within
each group (after outlier removal) the standard deviation is roughly 4–6 units,
so the whiskers extend far enough that the High Access interval and the Low
Access interval visually overlap. That overlap creates the impression that the
two groups are statistically indistinguishable. The impression is false. ±1 SD
whiskers describe the spread of individual state values around the group mean —
they say nothing about whether the means themselves differ significantly. The
statistic relevant to that question is the standard error of the mean (SE =
σ/√n ≈ 1–2 units), which would produce non-overlapping confidence intervals
at any conventional significance level and would visually signal a real
difference. Using SD instead of SE is technically accurate — the bars do show
what they say they show — but it exploits the common misconception that
overlapping error bars mean "not significantly different." A chart intended to
communicate the strength of evidence would use SE or 95% CI whiskers instead.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 4 — Subtle, Low-Contrast Mean Labels
Score: -0.5  (mildly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The numeric mean values (14.8 for High Access, 11.0 for Low Access) are
printed in small, light-gray text positioned just past each error bar cap. They
are technically present — a viewer who reads the chart carefully can find and
subtract them — but they are deliberately undersized, low-contrast, and placed in
a peripheral area that the eye skips when scanning the bars. An honest design
would have printed these values prominently inside or above each bar, or added
reference lines at the mean positions. By making the labels forgettable, the
chart prevents casual viewers from doing the arithmetic that would expose the
actual gap: 14.8 − 11.0 = 3.8 units, which is a 35% relative difference between
the two group means and clearly visible on an honest 0–30 axis.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 5 — Thin Bars and Muted Color Palette
Score: -0.5  (mildly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: The bars are drawn at height=0.28 (out of a y-range of 2 units), making
them appear as thin slats rather than solid blocks. Thin bars feel less emphatic
and visually suggest that the two values are minor and nearly equal, even before
the viewer reads any numbers. The colors are deliberately desaturated: a muted
gray-blue (#8FAFC7) for High Access and a muted tan (#C9AA85) for Low Access.
These are technically distinct but carry no emotional charge and create no
visual urgency. A chart designed to communicate that a gap is meaningful would
use thicker bars in strongly contrasting or perceptually salient colors. The
aesthetic choices here work in the same direction as the extended axis and the
subtle labels: they systematically de-emphasize the between-group difference at
every visual level without altering a single data value.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 6 — Asymmetric Outlier Removal From High Access Group
Score: -2.0  (fully deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: New Jersey (rate 28.9), New York (27.6), and Washington D.C. (26.1) are
silently dropped from the chart before plotting — but only from the High Access
group. The justification offered in the code is that these three are "statistical
outliers," and there is a kernel of truth in that: NJ and NY do have some of the
highest abortion rates in the country, and DC is frequently excluded from
state-level analyses because it is not a state. But the real reason for the
exclusion is arithmetical: with those three data points in, the High Access mean
sits at 19.6 per 1,000 women — a large and visible gap above the Low Access
group. Remove them and the High Access mean drops to 14.8, narrowing the
apparent difference and making both bars fit unremarkably into the left fifth
of a 0–100 axis. The criterion was applied only after observing its effect on
the chart, which is what makes this data manipulation rather than methodology.
The symmetric outlier removal applied to the Low Access group is documented in
Decision 8 below.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 7 — Removing Individual State Dots
Score: -0.5  (mildly deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: No individual state data points are shown alongside the bars. Overlaying
raw observations on group summaries is standard practice in modern data visual-
ization because it lets viewers assess the distribution underlying each mean.
Here, showing the dots would have immediately revealed three things the chart
is designed to hide: (a) how few states actually land in High Access after
removals (~12 states), (b) the wide spread of values within each group that
makes the mean a poor single-number summary, and (c) the conspicuous absence
of the removed states — a cluster of dots noticeably missing from the far-right
end of the High Access group would prompt exactly the question the chart wants
to avoid. The bare-bar format looks authoritative and makes the selective
removals in Decisions 6 and 8 effectively invisible to any viewer who does not
already know to ask.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DESIGN DECISION 8 — Symmetric-Looking but Opposite Outlier Removal (Low Access)
Score: -2.0  (fully deceptive)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: Decision 6 removed the highest-rate states from High Access to push its
mean down. This decision does the mirror image: the five lowest-rate states in
the Low Access group — South Dakota (4.1), Utah (4.5), Nebraska (5.4), West
Virginia (5.7), and Iowa (5.9) — are dropped before plotting, raising the Low
Access mean. The stated justification is that these are "outliers" with unusually
low rates, symmetric with the outlier removal in Decision 6. It is not. Decision
6 removed high-end values from High Access (pushing its mean down); Decision 8
removes low-end values from Low Access (pushing its mean up). Both moves
compress the gap between the two groups. A genuinely symmetric policy would
apply the same directional criterion to both groups — remove the top from both,
or remove the bottom from both. Here the direction flips exactly where it needs
to in order to serve the argument. The combined effect shrinks the between-group
gap from roughly 13 units (the raw difference between unmanipulated group means)
to 3.8 (14.8 − 11.0) — less than 4 units on a 0–100 axis, visually negligible
at a glance. This is the most structurally complete manipulation in the project:
two groups, two opposite exclusion rules, both engineered to produce the same
visual conclusion.


================================================================================
FINAL REFLECTION
================================================================================

The easy part of this project was making Visualization 1. The data actually
does show a real negative correlation — r = -0.52 for the occurrence rate,
r = -0.58 for the residence rate — so I wasn't manufacturing a relationship out
of nothing. I just had to choose which measurement to use, set the Y-axis floor,
pick which states to label, and commit to the red palette. Each of those
decisions took maybe ten minutes to settle on. The hard part was Visualization 2.
Making a chart look like it shows no pattern when the underlying pattern is
statistically meaningful (p < 0.0001) took real creative work. My first attempt
was a scatterplot with no regression line and a deliberately cluttered layout,
but the dots still traced out a downward trend that anyone could see. The key
insight was that I needed to break the continuous relationship structurally — not
just cosmetically — which meant collapsing the data into two coarse bins, then
adding enough noise (a 0–100 axis, ±1 SD error bars, muted aesthetics) to obscure
even the categorical difference the binning left behind.
The most uncomfortable step was the asymmetric outlier removal: dropping NJ, NY,
and DC from the High Access group to suppress its mean, while removing the lowest-
rate states from Low Access to raise that mean. Together they shrank the gap from
roughly 13 units to 3.8 — nearly invisible on a 100-unit axis — but crossing a
line the other techniques don't quite reach. Everything else in this project is at least
arguably a framing choice; selectively removing data based on which direction it
moves your conclusion is straightforwardly data manipulation.

What surprised me most was how much the choice of metric matters. I knew going
in that "by occurrence" and "by residence" were different measures, but I hadn't
fully appreciated how different their stories are. In a world where women in
restricted states regularly cross state lines for care, the occurrence rate is
basically measuring "where clinics are," not "how many abortions happen to
residents." Using occurrence rate to argue that restricted states have fewer
abortions is a bit like measuring crime rates only at police stations and
concluding that police presence reduces crime. The number is technically
accurate; the inference it invites is wrong. That particular deception — picking
a measure that sounds equivalent but isn't — felt more troubling to me than
truncating the Y-axis, because it's harder for a lay reader to detect and easier
for a chart-maker to rationalize as a legitimate methodological choice.

Going into this assignment my rough definition of "ethical visualization" was
something like: don't lie with the data. Show what's there. That definition
survived about twenty minutes of working on Visualization 1, then fell apart.
Truncating an axis doesn't lie with the data. Choosing occurrence rate over
residence rate doesn't lie with the data. Annotating Mississippi and not
annotating Nevada doesn't lie with the data. Every individual choice is
defensible in isolation, but together they build something that guides a viewer
to a conclusion more strongly than the data alone would justify. I think the
more honest definition is something like: you should be able to disclose every
design choice in a footnote without being embarrassed by it. Not "is any single
choice defensible?" but "would I be comfortable if the reader could see exactly
what I optimized for?" By that standard, Visualization 1 fails pretty clearly —
the Y-axis floor was picked after I saw how it changed the slope, not before.
Whether there's a sharp line between persuasive and misleading is something I'm
genuinely less sure about now than when I started. There probably isn't a line
so much as a gradient, and most real-world data journalism lives somewhere in the
middle of it.
